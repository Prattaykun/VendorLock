VendorLock AI Trade Intelligence Platform for FMCG & HORECA Distribution
One-Line Pitch
VendorLock is a chat-first AI trade intelligence platform that turns every order, payment, scheme, visit and dispute in India’s FMCG and HORECA supply chains into structured intelligence — builds a CIBIL-style trust score for every trade relationship, detects scheme leakage, fake returns, coverage gaps and expiry risk in real time, and tells distributors exactly who to trust, what to pre-stock and where they are losing money before the losses happen. For MVP, VendorLock runs on Telegram; the architecture is channel-agnostic so WhatsApp Business API can be plugged in next with no core changes
Meet Ravi — The Story Behind The Product
Ravi runs a mid-sized FMCG distribution business in Patna. He supplies 400 kiranas across 6 routes with 5 salesmen. Annual turnover: about ₹4 crore. He has been in business for 11 years.
Ravi’s Monday morning
 7 AM: 60 chat messages flood in on Telegram and WhatsApp in Hindi, Bhojpuri, broken English. His accountant manually logs them into Excel at noon.
 8 AM: Salesman Rahul leaves for his beat — 25 outlets on paper. He will mark all 25 as “visited” by evening. 6 were skipped. Ravi never knows.
 10 AM: Retailer Mahesh places a ₹12,000 order on credit. Mahesh already owes ₹48,000 from 45 days ago. Ravi approves on instinct.
 12 PM: A Surf Excel scheme: buy 100 units, get 12% discount. Ravi gets 12%. He passes 7% to retailers. 5% is silently absorbed in the chain. Nobody tracks where it went.
 3 PM: A biscuit batch expires in 88 days — within the 90-day return window. Ravi doesn’t know. The window closes in 2 days. ₹18,400 will become a write-off.
 6 PM: Three retailers cut orders this week. Ravi assumes “slow season”. In reality, quick commerce launched in nearby pin codes last month, undercutting his kirana customers on key SKUs.
By end of Monday, Ravi has lost
 Missed revenue from Rahul’s skipped outlets.
 Near-expiry stock heading toward write-off.
 Fresh credit extended to an already risky buyer.
 Scheme benefit never fully passed to retailers, weakening long-term trust.
 Several hours of his accountant’s time on manual entry.
VendorLock fixes each of these without Ravi changing how he prefers to work — messaging from his phone, using the same chats he uses today.
WHY — The Problem
India’s FMCG market is roughly ₹8.5–9 lakh crore annually, with around two-thirds of this flowing through general trade — kiranas, wholesalers and distributors — not supermarkets or modern retail. HORECA (hotels, restaurants, cafés, cloud kitchens) adds another huge, largely analog layer on top.
In this world, every order, payment, scheme, return and complaint travels through chat messages, voice notes, handwritten diaries and Excel files. Nothing is structured, searchable or shared in real time. Credit decisions run on memory and gut feeling. Scheme economics are opaque. Quick commerce is eating margins before distributors realise what changed.

What VendorLock Does
VendorLock is not a full ERP or payroll system. It is a chat-native intelligence layer that sits on top of existing ordering channels and ledgers. We group capabilities into:
 Core Distributor Intelligence (MVP + v2)
 Adjacent Verticals and Threat Intelligence (v2+) 1. Captures All Trade on Chat — In Any Indian Language (MVP)
Retailers continue messaging as they do today — in Hindi, Bhojpuri, Tamil, Bengali, Marathi, English or Hinglish. VendorLock’s Agent 1 reads each Telegram/WhatsApp message, extracts the order/payment/return/dispute intent, logs it to a structured ledger, and sends a confirmation back to the retailer in seconds. No forms, no app install.
Example chat: “bhai 50 Surf 1kg aur 30 Maggi bhej do, udhaar pe” VendorLock parses and logs: { Kiran Stores | Surf 1kg × 50 | Maggi × 30 | payment_type: credit | timestamp: 7:14 AM }
Auto-reply: “Order confirmed. Credit balance: ₹32,000 of ₹40,000 limit.”
MVP note:
 Channel: Telegram Bot for both distributor and retailers.
 The same logic later connects to WhatsApp Business API with identical message schema.
2. Builds a CIBIL-Style Trust Score for Every Trade Relationship (MVP)
Every payment, order, return and dispute feeds into a Trust Score from 0–100 for each retailer, HORECA buyer and distributor. Good payers earn better credit terms and priority service; risky buyers are flagged before outstanding amounts become losses.
Trust Score factors (example weights):
 Payment discipline (on-time vs delayed vs defaulted) – 30%
 Order consistency (regular, predictable behaviour) – 20%
 Cancellation rate (last-minute changes, no-shows) – 15%
 Return frequency (vs peer average) – 15%
 Communication reliability (response time, confirmation behaviour) – 10%
 Trade stability (length and continuity of relationship) – 10%
Score tiers:
 80–100: Tier A — trusted; highest limits and most flexible terms.
 60–79: Tier B — reliable; standard limits and normal terms.
 40–59: Tier C — caution; reduced limits and close monitoring.
 0–39: Tier D — risk; cash-only or credit freeze.
Retailers can query “MYSCORE” via chat anytime to see their own score and tier. 3. Detects Scheme Leakage in Real Time (v2 core)
VendorLock knows what scheme benefit came in from each brand and what went out to each retailer. It tracks pass-through and flags gaps per order, per customer, per month.
Example:
 Brand scheme: “Buy 100 units Surf Excel → 12% discount.”
 Distributor benefit: 12%.
 Benefit actually passed on to Kiran Stores: 7%.
VendorLock alert: “Scheme leakage: 5% (₹2,000) on this order. Month-to-date leakage: ₹47,000 across 14 retailers.”
4. Monitors Beat Coverage — Catches Ghost Visits (v2 core)
Every outlet should be visited or at least contacted on a known cadence. VendorLock combines the outlet master list, chat interactions and optional GPS pings to detect:
 Outlets not visited for N days.
 Salesmen who mark visits without any orders or 2-way chat.
 Sectors that are under-served for weeks.
Each morning, each salesman gets a chat message with:
 Ordered outlet list.
 Priority SKUs to push (near-expiry, high-margin, scheme-active).
 Risky or high-value retailers to focus on.
Example alert: “Sector 4 red for 3 weeks. Rahul has 6 check-ins with zero messages and zero orders. Likely ghost visits worth ~₹14,000 missed revenue this week.” 5. Catches Fake Returns and Protects Margin (v2 core)
Every return request is cross-checked against the original invoice and ledger:
 Quantity, batch number, purchase date, reason, supporting photo/voice note.
 Batch-level expiry data.
 Historical return pattern of that retailer.
VendorLock classifies returns as GENUINE / WITHIN_WINDOW / EXPIRED_WINDOW / SUSPICIOUS.
Example: Kiran Stores: “Return 40 units Maggi — near expiry.” VendorLock: purchased 4 months ago, expiry in 8 months, retailer has high recent return rate.
Alert: “₹1,800 credit note on hold. Claimed near-expiry does not match batch data. Review before approving.” 6. Tracks Near-Expiry Stock Before It Becomes Dead Inventory (v2 core)
Batch-wise expiry dates are captured from invoice photos on day one. VendorLock gives:
 90-day alerts: within brand return window, initiate claim.
 30-day alerts: push to top of beat plan and discount if needed.
 “What-if” projection: shows likely rupee loss if no action is taken.
Example alert: “Batch HUL2024B — 200 units biscuits. Brand return window closes in 2 days. Estimated value: ₹18,400. File claim today.” 7. HORECA Intelligence — Price, Reliability and Daily Spend (v2 adjacency)
For kitchens that order via chat, VendorLock:
 Parses daily orders across fresh produce, proteins and dry goods.
 Tracks vendor-wise price history and quality complaints.
 Builds vendor reliability scores and category-wise spend dashboards.
 Learns menu-linked demand patterns (e.g., paneer spikes Thu–Sun).
Example alert: “Chicken today billed at ₹195/kg vs 4-invoice average ₹180. Overcharge: ~₹750 on 50 kg. Vendor reliability score adjusted; consider re-negotiation.” 8. Quick Commerce Threat Monitor (v2 adjacency)
VendorLock’s Quick Commerce module (cron on Lambda or similar) tracks public prices on Blinkit/Zepto/Instamart for top SKUs in each distributor’s pin codes.
 Flags pin codes where QC undercuts retailer margin beyond a set threshold.
 Recommends redirecting push to safer regions or adjusting scheme support.
Example: “Maggi: QC price ₹12 in pin codes 800001–800009. Your average retailer margin falls below threshold. Shift Maggi push to Danapur and Phulwari where QC is not active.”
The “Lock” — Why VendorLock Is Hard To Leave
In general trade, retailers switch vendors quickly — for ₹1 lower price, slightly faster delivery, or a personal relationship. Loyalty is thin because there is almost nothing to lose by switching.
VendorLock changes that equation. Once a retailer has used VendorLock for 6–18 months, they have built up something genuinely valuable: a verified trade reputation that is recognised by distributors and lenders.
If they switch:
 Their full behavioural history remains inside the old network.
 They can carry a summary Trust Certificate to a new distributor, but the new distributor will still be more cautious until fresh on-platform behaviour is observed.
 They lose priority perks: higher credit limits, scheme access, 24-hour delivery, pre-orders on scarce SKUs.
Example — the ₹1 cheaper offer that isn’t worth it:
 Kiran Stores: Trust Score 88 (Tier A), credit limit ₹40,000, top scheme access, priority delivery.
 New distributor offers Surf Excel ₹1/unit cheaper but is not yet on VendorLock.
Switching outcome:
 Kiran can show a Trust Certificate, but the new distributor still caps first-month credit at, say, ₹5,000–10,000 until their own VendorLock-logged history is built.
 Priority delivery and scheme tier reset.
 Any NBFC loan evaluation that relied on the previous distributor’s long history may need re-verification.
For many retailers, ₹1 cheaper on a few cartons is not worth losing that earned reputation.

Fairness By Design — Switching & Protection
VendorLock deliberately creates switching costs without creating traps. The score belongs to the relationship, and a summary of that reputation belongs to the parties, not to VendorLock alone.
Scenario 1 — Mutual Agreement Switch
 Retailer asks current distributor for a Trust Certificate export.
 Platform generates a tamper-proof PDF: Trust Score, tier, months of history, payment discipline %, return rate vs peer average, consistency index, plus a QR code linking to a read-only verification page.
 New distributor (also on VendorLock) imports the certificate to set an initial tier above a typical new customer, then lets full on-platform behaviour rebuild the live score.
 Score continues building from that starting point.
Scenario 2 — Retailer Wants To Switch, Distributor Refuses
 Retailer can also request a basic Trust Certificate directly from VendorLock, verified via OTP on their phone.
 That certificate contains behavioural aggregates, not raw transaction logs, and can be shared with another distributor or NBFC.
 The new distributor sees enough signal to offer better terms than “stranger” status, but still needs time to fully trust the relationship.
Scenario 3 — Distributor Exploiting a Locked Retailer
To prevent abuse (e.g., distributor raising prices or quietly absorbing schemes knowing the retailer is “locked”), VendorLock supports a two-way Trust Score:
 Retailers can rate distributors on delivery punctuality, price consistency, scheme accuracy and complaint responsiveness.
 Distributor scores are visible to brands, NBFCs and prospective retailers considering joining that network.
 Chronic low scores or disputes raise questions for brands and finance partners, not just for the retailer.
Retailers can always see their own score and initiate a certificate export. They are invested in the system, not trapped by it.
Safeguards & System Integrity
Design principle: no participant should have unchecked power over another participant’s reputation, credit or livelihood.
Distributor-Side Safeguards
 Risk: Distributor manipulates a retailer’s Trust Score downward.
o Safeguard: Every score change must be backed by evidential anchors (chat logs, invoices, delivery records). Manual adjustments are impossible. Retailers get automatic notifications with a “DISPUTE” option; disputed changes are frozen until resolved.
 Risk: Fake retailer accounts created to absorb scheme benefits.
o Safeguard: New retailers require GSTN, Aadhaar-linked mobile or other strong ID. Schemes only activate after a minimum number of real orders. Outlets with one-way traffic (only scheme redemption, no normal orders) are flagged.
 Risk: False scheme documents uploaded.
o Safeguard: Scheme definitions are ingested from official brand PDFs or APIs where possible. All custom overrides require review. Unverified schemes stay inactive.
Retailer-Side Safeguards
 Risk: Score gaming — behave well for months, then default big.
o Safeguard: Credit velocity monitoring; sudden utilisation spikes or order-size anomalies trigger manual review regardless of score.
 Risk: Multiple accounts to reset a bad score.
o Safeguard: Identity anchoring via GSTN, Aadhaar-linked mobile and address. Cross-network defaulter flags propagate in anonymised form.
 Risk: Fake complaints against a distributor.
o Safeguard: Complaint weight is proportional to the complainant’s own Trust Score. A minimum number of independent complaints plus objective evidence is required before a distributor’s score is impacted.
Salesman-Side Safeguards
 Risk: Salesman pockets cash collections.
o Safeguard: Dual-confirmation payments; whenever a salesman logs a collection, the retailer receives a message: “₹X received from you. Reply YES or DISPUTE.” Unconfirmed collections are flagged.
 Risk: Salesman–retailer collusion on fake returns.
o Safeguard: No salesman can approve returns above a threshold; all such requests route to a distributor dashboard. Per-salesman return approval rates are tracked for anomalies.
AI & System Safeguards
 Risk: Model misreads a chat message.
o Safeguard: Confirm-before-commit; every parsed order is sent back to the retailer for confirmation before logging. Low-confidence parses are held for manual review.
 Risk: Wrong expiry date parsed from invoice.
o Safeguard: Expiry data is always surfaced in human-readable form for first-time confirmation; suspicious dates (already past) are not silently accepted.
Market & Platform Safeguards
 Risk: VendorLock becomes a data monopoly.
o Safeguard: Individual trade data is never sold. Only anonymised, aggregated insights are offered as a data service. Every user owns their data and can export/delete within policy.
 Risk: Dominant distributor blocks new entrants by refusing certificates.
o Safeguard: Retailer-initiated certificate export exists; distributor approval is helpful but not strictly required.
 Risk: New retailer has no history and is locked out of credit.
o Safeguard: Neutral starting score (e.g., 50/100) with a small “Trust Building” credit band universally available; existing high-score retailers can vouch, taking a small reputational stake.
 Risk: Large buyer bullies small supplier via unfair ratings.
o Safeguard: Right to respond for every negative rating; size-adjusted scoring so small suppliers are not evaluated by big-corporate benchmarks.
A short internal summary table can map each risk to its corresponding safeguard.

The 6 Agents — Technical Overview
This section is for your internal dev team; keep it concise and implementation-oriented.
Agent 1 — Trade Capture & Normalisation (MVP)
Purpose: Convert informal, multilingual chat messages into clean, structured trade records.
Inputs: Telegram/WhatsApp messages (text, Hinglish, regional languages), voice note transcripts, invoice photos.
Core logic: Intent classification (order / payment / return / dispute / scheme query) → entity extraction (items, quantities, unit prices, retailer ID, payment type, dates) → validation against product master → save to ledger.
Confirm-before-commit: Every parsed order is sent back to retailer for YES/DISPUTE before final logging.
Output: Structured event object with items, payment type, timestamp, channel, confidence score, status.
HORECA mode: Parses daily kitchen procurement orders similarly, with vendor_id and agreed price.
Agent 2 — Trust & Behaviour Scoring (MVP)
Purpose: Maintain a multi-factor Trust Score for every counterparty.
Inputs: Full ledger — payments, orders, returns, disputes, communication logs.
Logic:
 Compute weighted sub-scores for each behaviour dimension.
 Build a composite Trust Score plus 30/90-day trend.
 Compute a consistency index (sudden deviations penalised even before defaults).
 Adjust for seasonality by comparing retailers to their own historical baselines, not absolute thresholds.
Outputs: trust_score, tier, trend, flags (e.g., “2 late payments in last 30 days”), consistency_index.
Agent 3 — Risk, Scheme & Compliance Intelligence (Core v2)
Purpose: Combine Trust Scores, ledger data, scheme rules and compliance info to surface hidden risks and leakages.
Inputs: Trust outputs from Agent 2, full trade ledger, scheme PDFs (via RAG), batch/expiry data, vendor GSTIN + external compliance feeds.
Logic (five functions):
1. Credit risk detection — score trend + outstanding + order pattern.
2. Scheme leakage & pass-through tracking.
3. Returns & damage validation.
4. Expiry & near-expiry intelligence.
5. GST compliance flag per vendor.
Output: Structured alerts tagged INFO / WARNING / CRITICAL for Agent 4.
Agent 4 — Action & Recommendation (v2 core)
Purpose: Turn risk and intelligence signals into concrete, human-reviewable actions.
Inputs: Alerts from Agent 3 + distributor credit policy and risk appetite.
Outputs:
 Dashboard action cards.
 Chat-ready draft messages for collections, scheme explanations, credit decisions, vendor switches, brand return claims, Trust Certificates, etc.
Human-in-the-loop: Nothing executes automatically; distributor/manager reviews and approves.
Agent 5 — Demand & Pre-Stock Forecast (v2)
Purpose: Predict future demand per SKU per zone and recommend pre-stocking.
Inputs: Historical order series per SKU–retailer pair, seasonality calendar, active schemes, HORECA menu patterns.
Outputs:
 Demand spikes and drops by SKU/region.
 Pre-stock alerts for distributors.
 HORECA insights for kitchens.
Integration: Feeds Agent 2’s seasonality baseline and Agent 6’s beat planning (high-demand SKUs inserted into routes automatically).
Agent 6 — Beat Intelligence & Coverage (v2 core)
Purpose: Ensure salesmen visit the right outlets and prevent missed coverage.
Inputs: Outlet master list, check-in logs, order history per outlet.
Logic:
 Detect coverage gaps and ghost visits.
 Run shortest-path routing weighted by outlet priority and risk.
 Generate daily beat plans.
Outputs:
 Coverage gap alerts.
 Ghost visit alerts.
 Weekly missed revenue summaries.
 Salesman Reliability Score (ghost visit rate, collection confirmation rate).
Additional Modules — Technical Detail (v2+)
 Returns & Damage Reconciliation Engine — extension of Agent 3 with richer workflows.
 Quick Commerce Pressure Monitor — scheduled job and map visual.

Agent Orchestration Flow (Simplified)
1. Chat message received on Telegram (later WhatsApp).
2. FastAPI webhook passes to Agent 1.
3. Agent 1 parses, confirms with retailer, then commits to Postgres ledger.
4. Parallel triggers: Agent 2 updates Trust Score; Agent 3 evaluates risk and schemes.
5. Agent 3 pushes alerts to an alerts queue.
6. Agent 4 converts alerts into action cards and draft chat messages.
7. Distributor reviews actions, approves or edits.
8. Agents 5 and 6 update demand forecasts and beat plans in the background.

Final Architecture Summary
Channel: Telegram (MVP), WhatsApp Business API + web (roadmap). Frontend: React + TypeScript + Tailwind CSS. Backend: FastAPI. AI Pipeline: Bedrock-hosted LLMs + LangChain + AWS Strands for multi-agent orchestration. Core Agents: 6, plus 7 additional modules. Databases: PostgreSQL + MongoDB + vector store. Storage: S3. Jobs: Lambda + CloudWatch Events. Audit: SHA-256 hash chain (MVP) → ledger database (production). Infra: ECS + Docker. Interoperability: GSTN API, ONDC REST, NBFC partner APIs.
